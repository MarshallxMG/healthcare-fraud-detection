Thank you for giving me this opportunity. I would love to walk you through a project that I worked on called Healthcare Fraud Detection Using Machine Learning. This is a project that I am genuinely proud of because it sits at the intersection of data science and a very real, very serious problem that affects millions of people and costs healthcare systems billions of dollars every single year.

So let me start by giving you some context on why we chose this particular problem. Healthcare fraud is one of the largest sources of financial leakage in global healthcare systems. We are talking about fraudulent insurance claims, inflated billing, phantom services that were never actually provided, and upcoding where providers deliberately assign more expensive diagnosis codes than what the patient actually had. In the United States alone, the National Health Care Anti-Fraud Association estimates that healthcare fraud costs somewhere between sixty and two hundred billion dollars annually. In India, the problem is equally severe, especially as government-backed insurance schemes like Ayushman Bharat scale up. The victims here are not just insurance companies. When fraud goes undetected, premiums rise for honest patients, resources get diverted from people who genuinely need care, and the overall trust in the healthcare system erodes. That is what motivated us. We wanted to build something that could catch these fraudulent patterns early, accurately, and at scale, using the power of machine learning.

Now let me define the problem more precisely. Healthcare fraud comes in many forms. The most common ones include fake claims where a provider bills for services that were never rendered, upcoding where a provider submits a claim for a more expensive treatment than what was actually performed, duplicate claims where the same service is billed multiple times, and unbundling where a provider breaks a single procedure into multiple smaller ones to inflate the total cost. The challenge with detecting this kind of fraud is multi-layered. First, the volume of claims data is enormous. Even a mid-sized insurance company processes hundreds of thousands of claims every month, and manually reviewing each one is simply not feasible. Second, fraud is inherently rare compared to legitimate claims. In most datasets, fraudulent claims make up only a small percentage of the total, which creates what we call a class imbalance problem, and that makes it very difficult for traditional statistical methods to pick up on the fraudulent patterns. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the patterns of fraud are complex and constantly evolving. Fraudsters adapt their behavior, which means simple rule-based systems can only catch the most obvious cases. So the core problem we set out to solve was this: can we build an intelligent system that learns from historical claims data, identifies subtle patterns that distinguish fraudulent providers from legitimate ones, and flags suspicious claims in real time before they are paid out?

With that problem clearly defined, let me walk you through what we aimed to achieve. Our primary goal was to build a machine learning model that could accurately classify healthcare providers as potentially fraudulent or legitimate based on their claims behavior. But beyond just accuracy, we had several secondary goals that we considered equally important. We wanted high recall, meaning we wanted to catch as many actual fraud cases as possible, because a missed fraud case represents real financial loss. At the same time, we wanted to keep false positives manageable, because flagging too many legitimate providers would create unnecessary workload for fraud investigators and could damage relationships with honest healthcare providers. We also cared about interpretability. It is not enough for a model to just say "this is fraud." Auditors and investigators need to understand why the model flagged a particular provider, what specific patterns triggered the alert. And finally, we designed the system with scalability in mind. We wanted something that could be integrated into a real claims processing pipeline, not just a proof-of-concept notebook.

Now, let me talk about the data, because in any machine learning project, understanding your data is half the battle. We used a dataset sourced from Kaggle that contained real-world-style healthcare claims data. The original dataset came in multiple files, including beneficiary data, inpatient claims, outpatient claims, and provider-level labels indicating which providers were known to be fraudulent. After merging and preprocessing these files, we ended up with a consolidated dataset of approximately five hundred and fifty-eight thousand individual claims spanning about five thousand four hundred providers. Each claim record contained features such as the provider identifier, patient identifier, diagnosis codes in ICD-9 format, the claim type indicating whether it was inpatient or outpatient, the billed amount, the number of diagnoses listed, the number of procedures performed, the length of hospital stay, the patient's age, and the number of chronic conditions the patient had. Now, the data was far from clean when we first loaded it. There were missing values in several columns, particularly in fields like deductible amounts and procedure counts. There was noise in the data, with some claims having unrealistic values like negative amounts or ages above one hundred and twenty. And as I mentioned, there was significant class imbalance. These are all challenges that we had to address before we could even think about building a model, and I want to emphasize that understanding these data characteristics upfront saved us a lot of time and frustration later in the pipeline.

So let me explain how we prepared the data for modeling. The preprocessing phase was extensive and, honestly, it was where we spent a significant portion of our time. First, we handled missing values. For numerical features like deductible amounts, we used median imputation because median is more robust to outliers than mean. For categorical features, we used mode imputation or created an "unknown" category where appropriate. Next, we removed or corrected clearly erroneous records, such as claims with negative amounts or patients with impossible ages. We also dealt with duplicate records to ensure we were not double-counting any claims.

But the most critical preprocessing step, and I would say this was the single most impactful decision in the entire project, was our approach to feature engineering through provider-level aggregation. Instead of trying to classify individual claims as fraudulent, which is extremely difficult because a single claim in isolation tells you very little, we aggregated all claims at the provider level. So for each of the five thousand four hundred providers, we calculated statistics across all of their claims. We computed things like the total number of claims submitted, the number of unique patients treated, the mean and sum and standard deviation and maximum and minimum of the claim amounts, similar statistics for deductibles, number of diagnoses, number of procedures, and length of stay. We also created several derived features that turned out to be incredibly powerful. For example, we calculated claims per patient, which is the total number of claims divided by the number of unique patients. A provider who submits an unusually high number of claims per patient is behaving suspiciously. We calculated revenue per patient, which is the total billed amount divided by unique patients. We computed average diagnoses per claim as an indicator of upcoding, and we calculated the inpatient ratio, which tells us what proportion of a provider's claims are inpatient versus outpatient. In total, we engineered twenty-eight features for each provider. This aggregation approach is what took our model from a mediocre sixty-two percent accuracy when we were trying to classify individual claims to over ninety-four percent accuracy at the provider level. It was a game-changer because fraud is fundamentally a pattern of behavior, not a single transaction, and aggregating to the provider level allowed the model to see those patterns.

We also had to handle the class imbalance. About thirty-eight percent of providers in our dataset were labeled as fraudulent, which is actually a better balance than you see in many real-world fraud datasets, but we still used stratified sampling during our train-test split to ensure that both the training and testing sets maintained the same fraud ratio. We also explored the use of class weights in some of our models, particularly Random Forest with balanced class weights, to give the model a stronger signal about the minority class.

Now let me talk about model selection. We chose machine learning for this problem because, as I mentioned earlier, the patterns of fraud are complex, non-linear, and difficult to express as simple rules. Traditional rule-based systems can catch the obvious cases, like a claim amount that is ten times the expected price, but they miss the subtle patterns, like a provider who consistently submits claims with slightly elevated diagnosis counts across thousands of patients. Machine learning excels at finding these kinds of patterns in high-dimensional data.

We explored multiple modeling approaches. We started with a simple baseline to establish a benchmark. Then we moved to tree-based models, which are generally well-suited for tabular data with mixed feature types. Specifically, we trained and evaluated two main models. The first was a Gradient Boosting Classifier, which builds an ensemble of decision trees sequentially, where each tree corrects the errors of the previous ones. We configured it with two hundred estimators, a learning rate of zero point one, a maximum depth of six, and subsample ratio of zero point eight for stochastic gradient boosting. The second model was a Random Forest Classifier, also with two hundred trees, a maximum depth of ten, and balanced class weights. Random Forest builds trees in parallel, each on a random subset of the data, and then averages their predictions. Both models have their strengths. Gradient Boosting tends to achieve higher accuracy because of its sequential error-correction approach, while Random Forest with balanced weights can be better at handling class imbalance and is less prone to overfitting. We evaluated both models on the same test set and selected the one with the higher ROC-AUC score as our final model. In our case, the Gradient Boosting Classifier won.

Let me walk you through the training and evaluation process. We split our data eighty-twenty, with eighty percent going to the training set and twenty percent held out for testing. We used stratified splitting to maintain the class distribution. Before feeding the data to our models, we applied Standard Scaler to normalize all features to have zero mean and unit variance. This is important because our features had vastly different scales. The amount sum for a provider could be in the millions, while the inpatient ratio is a number between zero and one. Without scaling, features with larger magnitudes would dominate the learning process.

For evaluation, we deliberately chose metrics that go beyond simple accuracy. In fraud detection, accuracy alone can be extremely misleading. If ninety-five percent of your data is non-fraud and you build a model that simply predicts everything as non-fraud, you get ninety-five percent accuracy but you have caught zero fraud. That is useless. So we focused on precision, recall, and F1 score. Precision tells us, of all the providers our model flagged as fraudulent, how many actually were fraudulent. We achieved a precision of ninety-one point four percent, which means our false positive rate was manageable. Recall tells us, of all the providers that were actually fraudulent, how many did our model successfully catch. We achieved a recall of ninety-five point eight percent, which is excellent. It means we were missing very few actual fraud cases. The F1 score, which is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, came out to ninety-three point five percent. We also looked at the ROC-AUC score, which measures the model's ability to distinguish between the two classes across all possible threshold values. Our ROC-AUC was zero point nine six eight three, which indicates very strong discriminative ability. And to make sure our results were not just an artifact of one particular train-test split, we performed five-fold cross-validation. The results were consistent, with fold accuracies ranging from ninety-three point two five percent to ninety-five point one zero percent, and a mean of ninety-four point one four percent with a standard deviation of just one point four three percent. That consistency gave us confidence that the model generalizes well.

Now, looking at the confusion matrix in more detail, our model correctly identified six hundred and twenty legitimate providers as legitimate, and correctly flagged four hundred and six fraudulent providers as fraudulent. It misclassified thirty-eight legitimate providers as fraudulent, which are our false positives, and missed eighteen fraudulent providers, which are our false negatives. In fraud detection, we generally care more about minimizing false negatives because every missed fraud case represents real financial loss. Our false negative count of just eighteen out of four hundred and twenty-four total fraud cases in the test set is a strong result.

Let me share some insights we gained from the model's behavior. The feature importance analysis was particularly revealing. The top features driving the model's predictions were, in order: amount sum, which is the total revenue a provider brought in; revenue per patient; average claim amount; claims per patient; total claims count; chronic conditions sum; total diagnoses sum; amount per diagnosis; inpatient ratio; and average length of stay. What this tells us is fascinating. The model learned that fraudulent providers tend to have significantly higher total revenues and higher revenue per patient. They tend to submit more claims, bill higher amounts per claim, and attach more diagnoses per claim than legitimate providers. The inpatient ratio being in the top features suggests that fraudulent providers may be admitting patients unnecessarily or classifying outpatient visits as inpatient to charge more. These insights are not just academically interesting. They give fraud investigators concrete behavioral indicators to look for when they are auditing a provider.

I want to be transparent about the challenges we faced and how we addressed them. The first major challenge was the data imbalance issue I already discussed. We addressed it through stratified sampling, balanced class weights in Random Forest, and by choosing evaluation metrics that are appropriate for imbalanced classification. The second challenge was the risk of feature leakage. When you are building features from aggregated data, you have to be very careful that you are not inadvertently leaking the target variable into the features. For example, if the fraud label was somehow correlated with the order of claims in the dataset, and we used a feature that captured that order, the model would appear to perform well but for the wrong reasons. We carefully audited our feature engineering pipeline to prevent this. The third challenge was overfitting. Our training accuracy was one hundred percent while the test accuracy was ninety-four point eight two percent, a gap of about five percent. While this suggests some degree of overfitting, the cross-validation results showed consistent performance, so we concluded that the overfitting was within acceptable limits. We mitigated it through regularization parameters like minimum samples per split, minimum samples per leaf, and subsampling in Gradient Boosting. The fourth challenge was model interpretability. Black-box models are difficult to trust in regulated industries like healthcare. We addressed this by analyzing feature importances and building a two-layer detection system that combines interpretable rule-based logic with the ML model's predictions.

And that brings me to the real-world application aspect of this project, which I think is one of its strongest elements. We did not just train a model and generate metrics on a notebook. We built a complete, deployable system. The backend is built with FastAPI in Python, which provides a RESTful API that can receive claim data and return fraud assessments in real time. The frontend is a React application built with Vite, giving fraud analysts a clean, interactive dashboard to view statistics, analyze individual claims, and review analytics. The system uses a two-layer detection architecture. The first layer is rule-based. When a new claim comes in, the system first looks up the expected price for that specific diagnosis code from a database of over six thousand diagnosis codes with pre-calculated base prices. It then applies a provider type multiplier, because a private hospital naturally charges more than a government hospital, and classifies the claim into a pricing zone: normal, elevated, or suspicious. It also runs a series of heuristic rules to catch obvious anomalies, like an inpatient claim with zero days of stay, or a young patient listed with an unrealistic number of chronic conditions. The second layer is the machine learning model, which analyzes the subtler patterns. The two layers work together, with the rule-based layer catching the obvious cases quickly and the ML layer providing deeper pattern analysis. The final fraud probability is a combination of both layers.

In terms of integration and scalability, the API-based architecture means this system can be plugged into any existing claims processing pipeline. An insurance company could route incoming claims through our API endpoint before approving payment. The model is serialized as a pickle file that is only about one megabyte, so it loads quickly and makes predictions in milliseconds. The system also stores all user-submitted analyses in a local SQLite database, creating an audit trail that is essential for compliance in the healthcare industry. We even implemented GST calculation for the Indian healthcare context, which shows attention to real-world deployment requirements.

Now, I want to be honest about the limitations of our current system. First, our model is trained on a specific dataset that, while large and realistic, may not capture all the diverse patterns of fraud that exist in different healthcare systems across different regions and specialties. A model trained on data from one country or one insurance scheme may not generalize perfectly to another. Second, our current system analyzes claims individually at prediction time, whereas in an ideal scenario, we would continuously update provider profiles as new claims come in and detect shifts in behavior over time. Third, we used traditional machine learning models. There is potential to explore more advanced approaches like deep learning for sequence modeling of claims over time, or graph-based methods that could capture the complex relationships between providers, patients, and referring physicians to detect coordinated fraud rings. Fourth, while we have feature importance analysis, we could benefit from more sophisticated explainability tools like SHAP values or LIME to provide claim-level explanations that are more intuitive for non-technical auditors.

For future improvements, we would like to incorporate more data sources, including provider networks, referral patterns, and geographic information, to build richer profiles. We would also like to build a real-time streaming pipeline, perhaps using Apache Kafka, that processes claims as they arrive rather than in batch mode. Adding a feedback loop where fraud investigators can confirm or reject the model's predictions and have that feedback used to retrain and improve the model over time would be extremely valuable. And exploring graph neural networks to detect coordinated fraud across multiple providers and patients is an exciting direction for future research.
